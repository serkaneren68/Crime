\begin{abstract}
This work investigates the structure of the UCI \emph{Communities and Crime} dataset through a sequence of exploratory, statistical, and dimensionality-reduction analyses. The 122 predictive attributes of the dataset are first reduced to a working set of forty-three normalized sociodemographic variables through a label-blind, data-driven feature selection pipeline: low-coverage LEMAS attributes are dropped, and the remaining predictors are grouped by agglomerative hierarchical clustering on absolute Pearson correlation distance ($d = 1 - |r|$, complete linkage), from each cluster one representative being retained. The continuous target \texttt{ViolentCrimesPerPop} is discretized into Low, Medium, and High crime classes by tertiles. The pipeline includes per-feature box-plot inspection and Isolation-Forest-based multivariate outlier cleaning, Pearson and Spearman correlation analysis, z-score normalization with per-feature Fisher Distance, principal component analysis (PCA) with Fisher Distance per principal component, linear discriminant analysis (LDA), and a comparison of K-Means, DBSCAN, t-SNE, and Self-Organizing Map projections. Quantitative separability is reported through silhouette and 5-NN cross-validation accuracy on the 2-D embeddings, and the SOM organisation is summarised by a per-neuron class-purity hit-map. The analysis shows that family-structure and socioeconomic representatives (e.g., \texttt{PctKids2Par}, \texttt{PctIlleg}, \texttt{PctPopUnderPov}) carry the strongest individual class signal, that the leading PCA component inherits most of this discriminative content, and that LDA produces a tighter class-separable 2-D embedding than PCA. A Welch's t-test on \texttt{PctKids2Par} between Low and High classes establishes a highly significant mean difference at both $n=36$ and $n=64$.
\end{abstract}

Communities and Crime, exploratory data analysis, hierarchical clustering, feature selection, Fisher Distance, PCA, LDA, K-Means, DBSCAN, t-SNE, Self-Organizing Map, hypothesis testing.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
\PARstart{T}{he} \emph{Communities and Crime} dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository \cite{uciccrime} is a widely used benchmark for sociodemographic modelling of community-level violent crime in the United States. It aggregates information from the 1990 U.S.~Census, the 1990 U.S.~Law Enforcement Management and Administrative Statistics (LEMAS) survey, and the 1995 FBI Uniform Crime Reports (UCR) \cite{redmondbaveja}, and contains $1\,994$ communities described by $128$ attributes. Each predictive attribute is already normalized to the $[0,1]$ interval by the dataset authors so that comparisons across communities of different population are meaningful.

The aim of this study is not to build a competitive predictive model, but to characterize the statistical structure of this real, mixed-quality dataset through a sequence of complementary exploratory, dimensionality-reduction, and inferential analyses, and to interpret what each method exposes about the data. A central methodological choice is to replace subjective hand-curation of the working feature set with a fully data-driven, label-blind dimensionality reduction. Concretely, low-coverage attributes are first dropped, then the remaining predictors are grouped by agglomerative hierarchical clustering on the absolute Pearson correlation distance, and one representative per cluster is retained. This procedure produces a working set of $43$ sociodemographic features that spans every conceptual block of the original dataset --- demographics, ethnic composition, urbanization, income and housing cost, education, employment, poverty, family structure, immigration, housing quality, and residential mobility --- while bounding the within-cluster pairwise correlation. The selection mechanism is described in Section~\ref{sec:dataset}. To cast the problem in a multi-class setting, the continuous response \texttt{ViolentCrimesPerPop} is discretized into three equal-frequency classes \emph{Low}, \emph{Medium}, and \emph{High} by its tertiles.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:dataset} describes the dataset, the missingness filter, and the dendrogram-based feature selection. Section~\ref{sec:boxplot} examines the feature distributions and performs multivariate outlier removal. Section~\ref{sec:corr} analyzes the correlation structure among features and with the class label, and Section~\ref{sec:fisher} quantifies the per-feature discriminability through the Fisher Distance. Sections~\ref{sec:pca}--\ref{sec:pcscatter} study the principal-component structure and its low-dimensional class projections, and Section~\ref{sec:lda} contrasts these with a supervised linear discriminant embedding. Section~\ref{sec:cluster} probes the unsupervised cluster structure with K-Means, DBSCAN, t-SNE, and a Self-Organizing Map, and Section~\ref{sec:hypothesis} tests the significance of the leading predictor. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes the main findings.

\section{Dataset and Feature Selection}
\label{sec:dataset}
The Communities and Crime dataset \cite{uciccrime} contains $1\,994$ communities described by $128$ columns: five non-predictive identifiers (\texttt{state}, \texttt{county}, \texttt{community}, \texttt{communityname}, \texttt{fold}), $122$ candidate predictors, and the goal attribute \texttt{ViolentCrimesPerPop}. All predictive variables are already normalized to $[0,1]$ by the dataset authors through an unsupervised equal-interval binning that clips values beyond $\pm 3$ standard deviations; this normalization preserves the within-variable distribution shape but does not preserve relative magnitudes across variables. The continuous response \texttt{ViolentCrimesPerPop} is discretized into three equal-frequency classes \emph{Low}, \emph{Medium}, and \emph{High} by its tertiles, yielding $679 / 662 / 653$ samples before any cleaning. All processing was carried out in Python~3.10 using \texttt{NumPy}, \texttt{pandas}, \texttt{scikit-learn} \cite{pedregosa}, \texttt{scipy}, \texttt{seaborn}, and \texttt{MiniSom} \cite{minisom}.

\subsection{Identifier Removal and Missingness Filter}
\label{ssec:miss}
The five identifier columns were dropped first. The dataset then contains a contiguous block of $22$ variables derived from the LEMAS police-force survey (e.g., \texttt{LemasSwornFT}, \texttt{PolicPerPop}, \texttt{RacialMatchCommPol}) that is missing for $1\,675$ of $1\,994$ communities, i.e., for $84\%$ of the sample. Imputing such a deep gap would inject substantial artefactual variance and contaminate any downstream covariance-based method (PCA, LDA, Fisher Distance); these $22$ columns were therefore removed entirely. The remaining $100$ predictors carry at most $0.05\%$ missingness each and were imputed by the per-column median.

\subsection{Feature Selection via Hierarchical Clustering on Correlation Distance}
\label{ssec:fselect}
The $100$ surviving predictors still contain heavy conceptual redundancy: \texttt{medIncome}, \texttt{medFamInc}, \texttt{perCapInc}, \texttt{whitePerCap}, and the owner-occupied housing value quartiles \texttt{OwnOcc\{Low,Med,Hi\}Quart} all measure the same underlying construct (community wealth) at pairwise $|r| > 0.85$. A hand-curated working set inevitably reflects the analyst's prior expectations; we therefore replace it with an entirely label-blind procedure based on agglomerative hierarchical clustering \cite{hastie} of features in their pairwise-correlation geometry. Because the class label is never consulted during selection, the downstream supervised analyses (Fisher Distance, LDA, K-NN separability) remain free of selection-induced data leakage.

Two features that carry the same information must be treated as redundant regardless of the sign of their correlation: \texttt{racePctWhite} and \texttt{racepctblack} correlate at $r \approx -0.85$ but encode a single ethnic-composition axis. We therefore use the absolute-value Pearson correlation distance
\begin{equation}
d_{ij} \;=\; 1 - |r_{ij}|, \qquad d_{ij}\in[0,1],
\label{eq:corrdist}
\end{equation}
which maps both perfectly positively and perfectly negatively correlated pairs to $d=0$ and statistically independent pairs to $d=1$. The condensed pairwise distance matrix over the $100$ predictors is passed to agglomerative clustering with \emph{complete linkage}. Complete linkage merges two clusters only when the \emph{maximum} pairwise distance across them does not exceed the threshold, which guarantees that within every resulting cluster the pairwise correlation is bounded from below by $1 - d_{\mathrm{th}}$. Average and single linkage do not provide this guarantee and produce clusters that mix tightly and loosely correlated members.

A threshold sweep over $d_{\mathrm{th}}\in\{0.20, 0.25, \ldots, 0.60\}$ yields between $57$ and $29$ clusters, respectively. The operating point $d_{\mathrm{th}} = 0.35$ --- corresponding to a within-cluster correlation floor of $|r|\geq 0.65$ --- was retained. This choice produces exactly $43$ clusters, which is the smallest cluster count at which the four largest obvious redundancy blocks (income/wealth, family-structure, deprivation, and recent-immigration shares) consolidate into single groups, while conceptually distinct dimensions (e.g., immigration recency proportions vs.\ immigration share of population, or two-parent households vs.\ never-married mothers) remain separate. Figure~\ref{fig:dendrogram} shows the resulting dendrogram with the cut and the colour-coded clusters.

\subsection{Selection of a Cluster Representative}
\label{ssec:rep}
From each of the $43$ clusters exactly one representative is retained. For singleton clusters the representative is the cluster member itself ($24$ singletons). For each multi-member cluster, the default rule is to take the \emph{centroid} feature, i.e., the member whose mean pairwise correlation distance to the other members is lowest; this is the feature that best summarizes the cluster on its own. For $7$ of the $19$ multi-member clusters the centroid coincides with a feature widely used in the criminology and social-science literature and is kept as is. For the other $12$ multi-member clusters an interpretability-driven override was applied to favour the canonical literature variable over a near-equivalent centroid (e.g., \texttt{medIncome} is preferred to the centroid \texttt{RentHighQ} in the income-and-wealth cluster, since median income is the standard community-wealth proxy in the relevant literature). All overrides stay inside the cluster they replace, so the within-cluster correlation guarantee is preserved. The multi-member clusters and their final representatives are listed in Table~\ref{tab:clusters}.

\subsection{Final Working Set}
\label{ssec:final}
The procedure of Sections~\ref{ssec:miss}--\ref{ssec:rep} produces a deterministic working set of $43$ features --- $24$ from singleton clusters and $19$ from multi-member clusters --- listed by conceptual block in Table~\ref{tab:features}. Each retained feature represents a non-redundant axis of variation in the data, in the precise sense that no two retained features sit inside the same hierarchical-clustering cluster at the chosen correlation floor. Inter-cluster correlations are of course not bounded by this procedure: the cluster-12 representative \texttt{PctIlleg} and the cluster-6 representative \texttt{PctKids2Par}, for instance, still correlate at $|r|\approx 0.87$ because the dendrogram exposes them as two distinct but conceptually proximate ``concentrated disadvantage'' axes. This residual between-cluster correlation is itself a substantive observation about the dataset and is revisited in Sections~\ref{sec:corr} and \ref{sec:pca}. The same $43$-feature working set is used in every subsequent analysis.

\section{Feature Distributions and Outlier Removal}
\label{sec:boxplot}
Figure~\ref{fig:box_before} presents per-feature box plots of the $43$ selected variables before outlier removal, split into two panels and annotated by conceptual block for readability. Three distributional patterns are apparent. First, several features are strongly right-skewed with a long upper tail of extreme values --- most notably \texttt{population}, \texttt{PopDens}, \texttt{LandArea}, \texttt{NumImmig}, \texttt{PctIlleg}, and \texttt{PctVacantBoarded}; these tails reflect a small set of communities that are demographically or socioeconomically extreme (very large or dense cities, very high immigration counts, or concentrated deprivation). Second, the protective family and housing variables (\texttt{PctKids2Par}, \texttt{PctHousOwnOcc}, \texttt{PctEmploy}) are \emph{left}-skewed, with a long lower tail of disadvantaged communities. Third, \texttt{pctUrban} is markedly bimodal: its box spans almost the entire $[0,1]$ range because communities are predominantly either fully urban ($\approx 1$) or fully rural ($\approx 0$), a characteristic worth recalling when interpreting the projections of later sections. In contrast, \texttt{agePct12t29}, \texttt{agePct65up}, and the per-capita income variables are tightly concentrated around their median.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{boxplot_before.png}
\caption{Box plots of the $43$ selected features prior to outlier removal, grouped by conceptual block. Several socioeconomic variables show clear right-skew with long upper tails, the family/housing protective variables are left-skewed, and \texttt{pctUrban} is bimodal.}
\label{fig:box_before}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Why a multivariate detector.} A per-feature filter such as univariate IQR rejection discards any sample that is extreme on \emph{any} single dimension. This is doubly inappropriate here. Conceptually, several class-discriminative features (\texttt{PctPopUnderPov}, \texttt{PctIlleg}, \texttt{PctVacantBoarded}) intrinsically carry heavy upper tails populated almost exclusively by High-crime communities, so marginal trimming would deplete precisely the class the analysis is meant to characterize. Statistically, the probability that a row is flagged grows rapidly with the feature count, because a row is rejected as soon as it exceeds the threshold on \emph{at least one} of the $43$ marginals --- a direct manifestation of the curse of dimensionality. Table~\ref{tab:outlier} quantifies both effects: a per-feature IQR rule with multiplier $k=3$ removes $730$ rows ($36.6\%$ of the data) and collapses the High-crime class by more than half ($653 \rightarrow 295$, $-54.8\%$), which would render the supervised analyses of Sections~\ref{sec:fisher}--\ref{sec:lda} meaningless.

The final pipeline therefore uses the multivariate \emph{Isolation Forest} \cite{liu2008isolation} of \texttt{scikit-learn} \cite{pedregosa} with $200$ trees and a contamination rate of $5\%$. The detector isolates each sample by recursive random partitioning of the feature space; a sample that requires unusually few random splits to be isolated occupies a sparse region of the $43$-dimensional space and is flagged as a \emph{joint} anomaly. With contamination $=0.05$ the filter removes $100$ rows ($5.0\%$), leaving $1\,894$ communities with class distribution \emph{Low}: $660$, \emph{Medium}: $649$, \emph{High}: $585$ (Table~\ref{tab:outlier}). Notably, the High-class shrinkage is \emph{milder} on the $43$-feature set ($-10.4\%$) than on a smaller $18$-feature set ($-11.6\%$ in preliminary runs): the additional dimensions give the detector more evidence with which to separate legitimately extreme High-crime communities from communities that are genuinely anomalous across many axes simultaneously. This contrast has a substantive reading beyond data-cleaning hygiene: under a per-feature IQR rule the High-crime class collapses to less than half its size, whereas under a multivariate joint-anomaly criterion it survives almost intact. In other words, High-crime communities are \emph{extreme but not anomalous} --- they sit at the far end of an existing continuum of community characteristics rather than constituting a separate population of outliers, a point that the dimensionality-reduction analyses below will recover independently.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{boxplot_after.png}
\caption{Box plots after Isolation Forest cleaning (contamination $=0.05$). The bulk of each distribution is preserved, yet marginal outlier points (circles beyond the whiskers) remain visible --- a direct visual confirmation that the detector removes joint anomalies rather than per-feature extremes.}
\label{fig:box_after}
\end{figure}

A subtle but important confirmation is visible in Figure~\ref{fig:box_after}: many marginal outlier points (circles beyond the whiskers) \emph{persist} after cleaning. A per-feature IQR rule, by construction, would have produced boxplots with no points beyond the whiskers; their persistence here demonstrates that Isolation Forest targets samples that are anomalous in the \emph{joint} $43$-dimensional distribution, not those that are merely extreme on an individual axis. The qualitative shape of every feature distribution --- skew, median, and interquartile range --- is preserved, and all subsequent analyses use this cleaned dataset of $1\,894$ communities.

\section{Correlation Structure}
\label{sec:corr}
The full Pearson correlation matrix among the $43$ features and the ordinally encoded class label is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:corrheat}. With $44$ rows the per-cell numeric annotations would be illegible, so they are omitted and the conceptual-block boundaries of Table~\ref{tab:features} are drawn as black separators; the colour field alone then makes the macro-structure legible. The same two opposed super-blocks that the dendrogram of Section~\ref{sec:dataset} exposed re-appear here in correlation space. An \emph{advantage} super-block --- median income and the per-capita income variables, education (\texttt{PctBSorMore}), employment (\texttt{PctEmploy}), family stability (\texttt{PctKids2Par}), and home ownership (\texttt{PctHousOwnOcc}, \texttt{MedNumBR}) --- is internally positively correlated. A \emph{disadvantage} super-block --- poverty (\texttt{PctPopUnderPov}), illegitimacy (\texttt{PctIlleg}), overcrowding (\texttt{PctPersDenseHous}), and housing decay (\texttt{PctVacantBoarded}) --- is likewise internally positively correlated. The two super-blocks are strongly anti-correlated with each other, which is the well-known socioeconomic ``advantage--disadvantage'' gradient that the rest of this paper repeatedly recovers.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{correlation_heatmap.png}
\caption{Pearson correlation matrix of the $43$ features and the ordinally coded crime class. Numeric annotations are omitted for legibility; black lines mark the conceptual-block boundaries of Table~\ref{tab:features}. The opposed warm/cool blocks are the advantage--disadvantage socioeconomic gradient.}
\label{fig:corrheat}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{feature_class_correlation.png}
\caption{Per-feature Pearson correlation with the (ordinal) crime class, all $43$ features ranked. Red bars indicate positive correlation with higher crime levels, blue bars negative (protective) correlation.}
\label{fig:classcorr}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:classcorr} ranks all $43$ features by their correlation with the (ordinal) crime class. The strongest individual correlates are \texttt{PctKids2Par} ($-0.69$), \texttt{PctIlleg} ($+0.62$), \texttt{PctPopUnderPov} ($+0.52$), \texttt{PctPersDenseHous} ($+0.47$), \texttt{PctHousOwnOcc} ($-0.46$), \texttt{medIncome} ($-0.45$), and \texttt{PctVacantBoarded} ($+0.38$). The signs are consistent with the social-disorganization account of community-level crime \cite{shawmckay}, \cite{sampson}: family stability, income, education, and stable owner-occupied housing are protective, whereas poverty, family disruption, and housing decay co-occur with higher violent crime. Two observations are specific to the data-driven $43$-feature set. First, \texttt{PctPersDenseHous} (the share of people living in housing with more than one occupant per room) emerges as the fourth-strongest individual correlate. Importantly, the dendrogram of Section~\ref{sec:dataset} places it in a separate cluster from poverty and income, so overcrowding is empirically not redundant with deprivation; the signal it carries is best read as a distinct mechanism --- perhaps capturing dimensions such as informal housing, neighbourhood social stress, or routine-activity exposure --- that operates \emph{in addition to} pure economic deprivation. Second, the housing-quality block is collectively informative rather than incidental: \texttt{PctHousOwnOcc}, \texttt{PctVacantBoarded}, \texttt{PctHousOccup}, and \texttt{MedNumBR} all rank in the top ten. This suggests that the physical state of the housing stock tracks community crime nearly as closely as the canonical income and family variables, an observation worth highlighting because such structural housing measures are sometimes treated as second-order in the literature. At the opposite end, demographic and geographic variables --- \texttt{pctUrban}, \texttt{LandArea}, \texttt{agePct65up}, \texttt{householdsize}, \texttt{MedYrHousBuilt}, and the ethnicity-specific per-capita income variables --- carry essentially no individual class signal.

Because the class label is an \emph{ordinal} tertile rather than an interval-scale variable, the Spearman rank correlation was also computed for the feature--class relationship. The Spearman magnitudes are typically slightly larger (e.g., \texttt{PctKids2Par} $-0.70$, \texttt{PctIlleg} $+0.69$, \texttt{PctPopUnderPov} $+0.57$). The most pronounced shift is \texttt{PctPersDenseHous}, which rises from $+0.47$ (Pearson) to $+0.61$ (Spearman, third overall); the gap indicates that overcrowding relates to the crime tertile monotonically but non-linearly, consistent with a threshold effect in which crime rises sharply only once density passes a critical level. Despite such individual shifts, the absolute-correlation \emph{ranking} of features under the two metrics is almost identical (Spearman rank agreement $0.97$). This near-perfect rank agreement is itself a substantive finding: the qualitative conclusions of the entire correlation analysis are remarkably robust to the choice of association metric, holding under both linear and monotone formulations and therefore not depending on the parametric assumption of treating the ordinal class label as interval-scaled.

\section{Per-Feature Fisher Discriminability}
\label{sec:fisher}
After z-scoring, the per-feature Fisher Distance was computed as a multi-class generalisation of the classical two-class Fisher score \cite{fisher1936}:
\begin{equation}
J(f) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{c=1}^{C} n_c\,(\mu_c^{(f)}-\mu^{(f)})^2}{\sum_{c=1}^{C}\sum_{i\in c}(x_i^{(f)}-\mu_c^{(f)})^2},
\label{eq:fisher}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_c^{(f)}$ is the class-$c$ mean of feature $f$ and $\mu^{(f)}$ is its global mean. Larger $J(f)$ indicates that feature $f$ separates the class means well relative to within-class dispersion.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fisher_features.png}
\caption{Per-feature Fisher Distance after z-score normalization, all $43$ features ranked. \texttt{PctKids2Par} and \texttt{PctIlleg} dominate; roughly half of the features have Fisher Distance close to zero.}
\label{fig:fisher_feat}
\end{figure}

The resulting ranking (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher_feat}) is almost identical to the correlation ranking of Section~\ref{sec:corr}, which is expected: for a single z-scored feature, both the Fisher Distance and the absolute class correlation measure the contrast between class means relative to within-class scatter. The two top-ranked features are \texttt{PctKids2Par} ($J\!=\!0.90$) and \texttt{PctIlleg} ($J\!=\!0.69$), followed by \texttt{PctPopUnderPov} ($0.37$), \texttt{PctPersDenseHous} ($0.28$), \texttt{PctHousOwnOcc} ($0.27$), and \texttt{medIncome} ($0.26$). Two structural observations stand out. First, the discriminative content is sharply concentrated: \texttt{PctKids2Par} and \texttt{PctIlleg} dwarf every other feature, and there is a large drop to the third-ranked \texttt{PctPopUnderPov}, whose score is less than half that of \texttt{PctIlleg}. The two top features are highly correlated with each other ($|r|\approx 0.87$, Section~\ref{sec:dataset}) and most plausibly represent two complementary measurements of a single latent family-stability axis --- the share of children in intact two-parent households on one side and the share born outside marriage on the other. Treating the two as carrying independent signal would therefore overstate the dimensionality of the discriminative content; one underlying construct largely accounts for both. Second, roughly half of the $43$ features have a Fisher Distance below $0.03$ --- \texttt{pctUrban} and \texttt{PctUsePubTrans} are effectively zero ($J\approx 2\times 10^{-4}$), as are the housing-cost ratios \texttt{MedOwnCostPctInc} and \texttt{MedYrHousBuilt}. In other words, although the working set spans $43$ conceptually distinct axes, the single-feature class signal lives in only about six to ten of them. This concentration foreshadows the principal-component analysis of Section~\ref{sec:pca}: when most individual axes are class-uninformative and the informative ones are mutually correlated, a very small number of linear combinations should capture essentially all of the separability.

\section{Principal Component Analysis}
\label{sec:pca}
PCA \cite{jolliffe} was applied to the z-scored data, producing $43$ principal components. The leading component PC1 explains $18.6\%$ of the variance and PC2 a further $14.3\%$; the first five components together account for only $53.4\%$ of the total variance, and sixteen components are needed to reach $80\%$ (Figure~\ref{fig:scree}). The scree curve is therefore far flatter than it would be for a small hand-picked feature set: because the $43$ features deliberately span many weakly-correlated conceptual blocks, the variance is distributed across many directions rather than collapsing onto a single dominant axis.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{pca_scree.png}
\caption{PCA scree plot for the $43$-feature working set. PC1 captures $18.6\%$ of the variance and PC2 a further $14.3\%$; the top five components capture $53.4\%$, and sixteen are needed for $80\%$.}
\label{fig:scree}
\end{figure}

Treating each PC projection as a feature, the Fisher Distance from Equation~(\ref{eq:fisher}) was recomputed (Figure~\ref{fig:fishercmp}). The Fisher Distance of PC1 is $0.56$, comfortably the largest among the principal components; every other PC scores below $0.07$. Crucially, however, PC1's $0.56$ is itself \emph{lower} than the two best original features (\texttt{PctKids2Par} at $0.90$ and \texttt{PctIlleg} at $0.69$). The unsupervised leading component is thus a worse single-axis class discriminator than simply retaining the best raw feature, because PC1 is built to maximize total variance and necessarily blends the discriminative ``advantage--disadvantage'' direction with correlated but less discriminative variation. This is the central motivation for the supervised projection (LDA) of Section~\ref{sec:lda}.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fisher_comparison.png}
\caption{Fisher Distance per z-scored original feature (left) and per principal component (right), on a shared vertical scale. The per-feature signal is spread over several variables, whereas the per-PC signal is concentrated almost entirely in PC1.}
\label{fig:fishercmp}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{eigval_vs_fisher.png}
\caption{Eigenvalue versus Fisher Distance for the $43$ principal components. Pearson correlation $r=0.76$, Spearman $\rho=0.49$. PC2 has the second-largest eigenvalue yet a near-zero Fisher Distance.}
\label{fig:eigfisher}
\end{figure}

The relationship between the eigenvalue of a principal component (its variance) and its Fisher Distance is positive but weak and entirely concentrated in the leading component: the Pearson correlation is $r=0.76$ and the Spearman rank correlation is only $\rho=0.49$ (Figure~\ref{fig:eigfisher}). The high Pearson coefficient is an artefact driven solely by PC1, which sits in the far corner of the scatter with both the largest eigenvalue and by far the largest Fisher Distance; with PC1 removed, the eigenvalue--Fisher association essentially vanishes. The clearest illustration is \textbf{PC2}: it carries the second-largest variance ($14.3\%$) yet a near-zero Fisher Distance ($0.06$), and PC3 (third-largest variance) scores only $0.01$. These high-variance, class-irrelevant components are a direct demonstration of the textbook caveat that PCA maximizes variance, \emph{not} class separability: PC2 captures a large axis of community variation --- orthogonal to the crime gradient --- along which the three classes are essentially indistinguishable. Only PC1 happens to align with the discriminative direction, and even it does so imperfectly. This pattern should not be read as a failure of PCA. Rather, it is a substantive observation about the data: crime-relevant variation is only one component of total community variation, and other prominent axes (immigration and ethnic composition along PC2, see Appendix~\ref{app:pcaload}) coexist with it without driving the class label. Consequently, an unsupervised projection that maximizes total variance must spend some of its budget on these other axes, which is precisely what makes the supervised projection of Section~\ref{sec:lda} structurally better suited to class separation.

\section{Linear Discriminant Analysis and Class Separability}
\label{sec:lda}
A Linear Discriminant Analysis \cite{fisher1936} was fit with $n_{\text{components}}=2$. By construction, LDA maximizes between-class scatter relative to within-class scatter and therefore tends to produce a tighter class-separable 2-D embedding than PCA when class information is available.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{lda_scatter.png}
\caption{LDA 2-D projection. The three classes form a clearer left--right ordering than in the PCA projection.}
\label{fig:lda}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[!t]
\caption{Quantitative separability of the 2-D PCA and LDA embeddings. Higher is better in both cases.}
\label{tab:sep}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Metric & PCA (PC1,PC2) & LDA (LD1,LD2) \\
\midrule

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

To make the comparison quantitative, two metrics were computed on the 2-D embeddings (Table~\ref{tab:sep}). The silhouette coefficient \cite{rousseeuw} (using the true class labels as the partition) rises from $0.049$ for PCA to $0.127$ for LDA, an increase of roughly $160\%$ ($2.6\times$). The mean $5$-fold cross-validated accuracy of a $5$-NN classifier trained on the 2-D embedding improves from $56.9\%$ on PCA to $65.9\%$ on LDA, a gain of $+9.0$ percentage points. Both metrics confirm what Figures~\ref{fig:pcscat} and \ref{fig:lda} show: LDA reveals substantially tighter class separability than PCA. Importantly, this gap is \emph{wider} on the $43$-feature set than a low-dimensional analysis would suggest, and the reason follows directly from Section~\ref{sec:pca}. The PCA embedding must spend one of its two axes (PC2) on the largest available direction of variance, which Section~\ref{sec:pca} showed to be class-irrelevant; only PC1 carries class signal, so PCA effectively has a single useful discriminative axis. LDA, being supervised, orients \emph{both} of its axes to maximize between-class relative to within-class scatter, and therefore exploits the rich $43$-dimensional feature space far more efficiently. In short, PCA wastes half of its 2-D budget on class-irrelevant variance whereas LDA does not, and the metric gap quantifies exactly that difference.

\paragraph{K-Means} on the (PC1, PC2) plane recovers three clusters whose boundaries are roughly vertical cuts along PC1, echoing the gradient of Section~\ref{sec:pcscatter}. The agreement with the true labels (ARI $=0.14$, NMI $=0.15$) is far from perfect but is not negligible: even with no access to the labels, an isotropic partitioning of the PCA plane recovers a non-trivial fraction of the class structure, which by itself indicates that the leading socioeconomic gradient is strong enough to surface without supervision. The agreement is nonetheless capped because K-Means treats PC1 and PC2 isotropically while PC2 ($14.3\%$ of variance) is class-irrelevant, so part of the partitioning effort is necessarily spent splitting communities along a direction unrelated to crime. Unsupervised clustering in the PCA plane therefore inherits the dissociation identified in Sections~\ref{sec:pca}--\ref{sec:lda}.

\paragraph{DBSCAN} essentially fails: at $\varepsilon=0.5$, $\mathrm{minPts}=10$ it produces one dominant dense cluster, a handful of tiny fringe clusters, and $233$ noise points ($12.3\%$), for an ARI of only $0.018$. Because the data form a single continuous ``socioeconomic gradient'' rather than well-separated density modes, density-based clustering cannot recover the three classes; DBSCAN exposes that the class structure of this dataset is gradient-like, not modal.

\paragraph{t-SNE} on the full $43$-dimensional data produces a single, smoothly varying point cloud along which the three classes form a continuous gradient (\emph{Low} on one side, \emph{High} on the other) rather than separated islands. This reinforces the picture that the classes are largely defined by position along a one-dimensional socioeconomic continuum, not by compact modal clusters --- the same conclusion reached independently by PCA, DBSCAN, and the SOM.

\paragraph{SOM} The $12\times 12$ MiniSom grid (Figure~\ref{fig:som}) provides the most class-organised non-linear view. The hit-count heat-map (centre panel) shows that the SOM allocates capacity smoothly across the grid with a few slightly heavier hubs. The dominant-class panel (right) reveals contiguous regions of Low, Medium, and High classes arranged along a gradient on the lattice, with several cells reaching $\geq 90\%$ purity. The mean per-neuron purity over non-empty cells is $67.5\%$, well above the $\approx 35\%$ expected from chance under the (near-uniform) class distribution. The SOM thus confirms, on the original $43$-dimensional feature space, the same gradient-like organisation seen in the t-SNE map.

\section{Significance of the Leading Predictor}
\label{sec:hypothesis}
The feature \texttt{PctKids2Par} (percentage of kids living in two-parent households) was selected for the hypothesis test because it has the largest Fisher Distance and the largest absolute correlation with the class label (Sections~\ref{sec:corr}--\ref{sec:fisher}). The hypothesis to be tested is the equality of its mean between the \emph{Low} and \emph{High} crime classes:
\begin{align*}
H_0:\ \mu_{\text{Low}} = \mu_{\text{High}}, \qquad
H_1:\ \mu_{\text{Low}} \neq \mu_{\text{High}}.
\end{align*}
Two independent random samples of sizes $n=36$ and $n=64$ were drawn (without replacement) from each class, and a two-sided Welch's t-test \cite{welch} (which does not assume equal variances) was applied. The results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:ttest}.

\begin{table}[!t]
\caption{Welch's t-test results on \texttt{PctKids2Par} between the Low and High crime classes, with Cohen's $d$ as an effect-size measure (pooled within-group standard deviation).}
\label{tab:ttest}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
$n$ per class & $\bar x_{\text{Low}}$ ($\pm$ s.d.) & $\bar x_{\text{High}}$ ($\pm$ s.d.) & $t$ \ ($p$-value) & Cohen's $d$ \\
\midrule
$36$ & $0.759\pm 0.130$ & $0.463\pm 0.147$ & $9.01$ \ ($2.8\!\times\!10^{-13}$) & $2.12$ \\
$64$ & $0.792\pm 0.112$ & $0.444\pm 0.188$ & $12.73$ \ ($7.3\!\times\!10^{-23}$) & $2.25$ \\
all  & $-$              & $-$              & $39.28$ \ ($\approx 10^{-207}$)    & $2.28$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

In all three settings (samples of size $36$, $64$, and the full classes) the p-value is many orders of magnitude smaller than any conventional significance level $\alpha\in\{0.05, 0.01, 0.001\}$, so the null hypothesis of equal means is rejected with overwhelming evidence in favour of $H_1$. With near-population sample sizes, however, statistical significance alone is a weak summary, since any non-zero mean difference will eventually attain arbitrarily small p-values; the substantive question is the \emph{magnitude} of the difference. Cohen's $d$, reported in the last column of Table~\ref{tab:ttest}, is approximately $2.1$ at $n=36$, $2.3$ at $n=64$ and $2.3$ on the full classes --- effect sizes that exceed Cohen's conventional ``very large'' threshold ($d\!\geq\!1.2$) by a wide margin. The Low- and High-crime distributions of \texttt{PctKids2Par} are therefore not only statistically distinguishable but separated by more than two pooled standard deviations of within-class spread, on a normalized scale ($\approx 0.79$ vs.\ $0.45$, a shift of $\approx 0.33$ on $[0,1]$). As expected, increasing the sample size from $36$ to $64$ leaves the sample means and $d$ almost unchanged while increasing $|t|$ from $9.01$ to $12.73$, illustrating that what grows with $n$ is statistical power, not the underlying effect itself.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This study applied a complete exploratory and statistical analysis pipeline to the UCI Communities and Crime dataset. A label-blind, hierarchical-clustering feature selection reduced the $122$ predictors to a non-redundant working set of $43$ features, and the continuous violent-crime rate was discretized into three classes. Read across the eight analyses, the central finding can be stated in one sentence: \emph{violent crime risk in this dataset is organized predominantly along a single socioeconomic disadvantage continuum.} Pearson and Spearman correlation, per-feature Fisher Distance, principal-component analysis, linear discriminant analysis, and the four clustering methods all recover the same gradient with remarkable consistency, despite very different inductive biases. The remainder of this section elaborates this thesis through the more detailed findings of the analysis.

First, family-structure and socioeconomic representatives (\texttt{PctKids2Par}, \texttt{PctIlleg}, \texttt{PctPopUnderPov}, \texttt{PctPersDenseHous}, \texttt{PctHousOwnOcc}, \texttt{medIncome}) carry the strongest individual class signal, as confirmed independently by both Pearson and Spearman correlation and by per-feature Fisher Distance (Spearman--Pearson rank agreement $0.97$); the data-driven set also surfaces overcrowding (\texttt{PctPersDenseHous}) as a top correlate that a hand-picked set might have missed, while demographic and geographic variables such as \texttt{pctUrban}, \texttt{agePct65up}, and \texttt{LandArea} are essentially class-uninformative. Second, the discriminative content is concentrated in very few directions but no longer in a single dominant one: PC1 explains only $18.6\%$ of the variance and obtains a Fisher Distance of $0.56$ --- itself lower than the two best raw features --- while the second-largest variance component PC2 ($14.3\%$) is class-irrelevant (Fisher $0.06$). Eigenvalue and Fisher Distance are thus only weakly related ($r=0.76$ driven entirely by PC1, $\rho=0.49$): high variance does not imply high discriminability, a point made starkly by PC2. Third, the LDA 2-D embedding clearly outperforms the PCA 2-D embedding in revealing class separability, both visually and quantitatively (silhouette $0.127$ vs.\ $0.049$; 5-NN CV accuracy $65.9\%$ vs.\ $56.9\%$), precisely because PCA must spend one of its two axes on class-irrelevant variance whereas supervised LDA does not. Fourth, unsupervised clustering reveals that the class structure is gradient-like rather than modal: K-Means on the PCA plane recovers only modest agreement (ARI $0.14$), DBSCAN collapses to one dominant cluster with $233$ noise points (ARI $0.02$), and both t-SNE and SOM produce a continuous manifold along which the three classes shade smoothly into each other; nevertheless the SOM achieves a mean per-neuron class purity of $67.5\%$, well above the chance level. Finally, a Welch's t-test on \texttt{PctKids2Par} rejects the null hypothesis of equal class means with extreme significance at both $n=36$ ($t=9.01$) and $n=64$ ($t=12.73$); more informatively, the Cohen's $d$ effect size of $\approx 2.2$ places the gap between the Low- and High-crime distributions at more than two pooled within-class standard deviations, formally confirming and \emph{quantifying} the descriptive evidence of the earlier sections.

Overall, the dataset is best understood not as three well-separated clusters but as a one-dimensional socioeconomic continuum along which violent crime rises monotonically. Two further consequences of this geometry are worth noting. First, a comparison of the multivariate Isolation Forest filter with a per-feature IQR rule (Table~\ref{tab:outlier}) shows that High-crime communities are \emph{extreme but not anomalous}: a per-feature outlier rule discards more than half of them, whereas a joint-anomaly rule discards only one in ten, indicating that these communities sit at the far end of an existing continuum rather than constituting a separate population. Second, that the gradient is consistently the leading axis under every method explored explains why a supervised linear method (LDA) outperforms the unsupervised PCA projection, why density-based clustering fails, and why the strongest individual predictors are family-structure, deprivation, and housing-quality indicators rather than purely demographic ones. The data-driven $43$-feature selection both removes the subjectivity of manual curation and exposes structure --- such as the dissociation between PCA variance and class separability, the role of overcrowding as a mechanism distinct from poverty, and the collective informativeness of the housing-quality block --- that a smaller hand-picked working set would have obscured.

\section{Data and Code Availability}
\label{app:repro}
The analysis pipeline was implemented in Python~3.10 using NumPy, pandas, SciPy, scikit-learn~\cite{pedregosa}, seaborn and MiniSom~\cite{minisom}. The pipeline is organized in two stages: a label-blind feature-selection stage that performs the hierarchical clustering of Section~\ref{ssec:fselect} and emits the $43$-feature working set, and an analysis stage that consumes that selection and produces every figure and statistic reported above. Source code, generated figures and an interactive notebook are provided as supplementary material to permit independent verification of every reported number. The original dataset is publicly available from the UCI Machine Learning Repository~\cite{uciccrime}.
